Project 001

Chagan Ice Fishing Tent

The aim of this project was to design a shelter suited for the ice fishing season of Qian Gorlos Mongol Autonomous County, Jilin Province, China. The main requirements: Chagan Lake tents needs to be environmentally friendly, easily transportable and deployable, and resistant to harsh winter cold and wind. The resulting tent consists of two polyhedra structures: an elongated square cupola body and a gyroelongated square cupola skirt. The central advantages of this design were excellent wind deflection (faceted angles break gusts), strong load distribution (ideal for snow and ice pressure), and a naturally insulating form (low surface-to-volume ratio). Developed in collaboration with Dianne Cao (MIT) and Jingtong Yan (Harvard).

Project 002

Dragonflight Lamp

This lamp was designed to bring character and dynamicism to a popular study spot on MIT campus. Inspired by the delicate intricacy and refractory properties of dragonfly wings, this lamp comprises of a number of adjustable and playful features: the angle at which the wings are bent can be adjusted, wings can be removed and switched out, colors and brightness of the lamp light can be adjusted, lamp head can be rotated 360 degrees, and the base of the lamp can be spun. Developed and fabricated in collaboration with Stasya Selizhuk (MIT).

Project 003

Massachusetts General Hospital Ether Dome

This project was an exercise in primitives modeling in Rhino. Using basic polygons, polygon extrusions, solids, boolean union and difference functions, and array and symmetry functions, I modeled the MGH Ether Dome, the second best dome in the Boston-Cambridge area (we all know which building takes the gold).

Project 004

Parametric Spiral Staircase

A simple Rhino and Grasshopper-designed spiral staircase with a number of adjustable parameters, including staircase height, radius, resolution, number of steps, degree of rotation, railing thickness, number of railing poles, and inner stem radius.

Project 005

Parametric Ceiling Truss System

A ceiling truss system with a fully adjustable UV-curved nurb base. UV axes are uniquely defined for an individual surface, with adjustable and separate resolutions in both U and V directions, which are also customizable. The resulting ceiling surface base of this truss can be pulled and twisted any which way. It also includes other parametric features: number of trusses, style of truss, thickness of pipes, direction of trusses, and density of trusses.

Project 006

Metal Bookmarks

I am an avid reader (check out my StoryGraph @eunyounga). Paper bookmarks have always felt lacking in pizzazz for me, however. So I designed this set of steel, laser-cut bookmarks. Three of these bookmarks were hand-drawn in Illustrator, then prepared for laser cutting in Rhino. The other three, the patterned bookmarks, were created using a Python plug-in into Rhino, and are parametric and can be adjusted in terms of size, thickness, stretch, and more.

Project 007

The Gorgons

Tasked with the lofty mission of filling one of MIT's major event spaces, Lobby 13, with an installation that lived and breathed, my team and I designed, fabricated, and displayed an inflatable piece of massive proportions. Inspired by the myth of the Gorgons of Greek mythology, three sisters with snakes for hair, we decided to create a fantastical monster to invade the empty, sterile Lobby 13. This manifested as a set of serpentine, branching inflatables, each approximately 150 feet in length with arms spanning 8 to 12 feet, that encircled the pillars of Lobby 13, wrapping themselves around the stone when blown up. We designed the branches to be an interactive element with which the audience could engage by holding, wrapping, touching, and otherwise manipulating. We also included a number of free floating "eyes," clear balloons with miniature LED lights inside with helium content carefully calculated such that the balloons would freely bob without sinking to the floor nor rising to the ceiling to further propagate the feeling that the inflatable sculpture was alive. Created in collaboration with Dianne Cao (MIT) and Sarah Perez (MIT).

Project 008

Algorithmic Willow Tree

This willow tree was created as an exercise in more complex Grasshopper coding. I utilized simple solids and shapes in order to highlight the central feature of this project: using algorithmic extensions such as looping and randomization, this Grasshopper program produces a slightly different willow tree with customizable parameters each time it is run. The algorithm was designed with the natural random growing, branching behavior of real-life willows.
